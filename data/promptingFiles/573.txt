NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3080 Tested: Gamers Are Going To Love This Thing

Sep 16, 2020, 09:04am EDT

NVIDIA lifted the review embargo veil this morning on its new GeForce RTX 3080 graphics cards for gamers, and we can definitively say now that the company made good on the lofty performance claims that its CEO, Jensen Huang detailed upon its initial debut. The NVIDIA GA102 GPU powering the new RTX 3080 is based on the company’s all new Ampere GPU architecture that has been revamped and beefed-up in all the right places for a major lift in performance. In addition, NVIDIA re-engineered a great-looking new cooling solution for the GeForce RTX 3080, with a new push-pull, dual axial fan design that is claimed to keep the Ampere GPU 20C cooler under load and with a quieter acoustic signature as well.
And of course this new cooling solution was required because, even though Ampere is built on a new cutting-edge 8nm Samsung chip fab process, it is a beastly piece of silicon comprised of over 28 billion transistors (previous-gen Turing was just 13.6M), that’s also strapped with 10GB of new ultra-high speed GDDR6X memory. So, there’s serious horsepower on board here that needs to be kept cool under pressure. Regardless, NVIDIA hit it out of the park with this board design, and the new GeForce RTX 3080 is an engineering masterpiece that delivers a truly complete solution.
First, let’s wade into the weeds just a tiny bit on the RTX 3080’s core counts, speeds and feeds. Then we’ll look at what they do for performance. This spec table is a bit of an eye chart but it gets the job done and I actually culled it down a bit...
Don’t get too bogged-down in the first few lines of this chart. GPCs, TPCs and SMs are basically how compute resources are segmented and clustered together (Graphics Processing Clusters, Texture Processing Clusters and Streaming Multiprocessors). The key takeaways here for standard rasterization and shading, are that Ampere has a boatload more CUDA cores and texture processing versus the previous gen Turing architecture, and a larger number of Streaming Multiprocessors. However, those SMs are significantly beefed-up and enhanced with 2X the FP32 Floating Point throughput per clock, 2X the L1 cache bandwidth and a 33% larger L1 cache size. Ampere also has a larger number of 2nd gen RT (ray tracing) cores (68 vs 48 in an RTX2080 Super) and it can also perform RT and Shader operations concurrently, which Turing could not do. Ampere, however, has fewer Tensor cores for NVIDIA’s AI-enhanced DLSS image enhancement technology (and other machine learning-targeted math operations), though they are much more powerful, with up to 2X the throughput per core.
GPU clock speed for an RTX 3080 is slightly slower at 1710MHz, versus 1815MHz for a previous gen RTX 2080 Super. However, memory bandwidth is off the hook for the GeForce RTX 3080, at 760GB/s, versus 496GB/s for the 2080 Super. This is achieved via Micron’s GDDR6X memory that has a 19Gbps data rate over the RTX 3080’s wider 320-bit memory interface (256-bit for previous gen). GDDR6X also employs PAM4 signaling that transmits 2X the number of bits per clock tick.
So, at a high level, that’s Ampere and the new NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3080 – bigger, wider, faster and more powerful all around, with a much-improved cooling solution to boot. But how does this all translate performance-wise?
If I’m being honest, and that’s why you come here I hope, I don’t have enough time to game lately, but firing up NVIDIA’s new GeForce RTX 3080 might just suck me back in. Starting with some quick synthetic sanity checks, 3DMark Time Spy’s DirectX 12 benchmark shows the GeForce RTX 3080 crushing even the mighty NVIDIA Titan RTX by about 22 percent in its Graphics score, and it’s over 52 percent faster than a GeForce RTX 2080 Super.
Moving to ray tracing workloads with 3DMark Port Royal, that lead extends even further, where the RTX 3080 is roughly 64% faster than a 2080 Super.
Moving on to real gaming benchmarks, Metro Exodus with its standard Ultra quality preset, shows the new Ampere card laying to waste all competition, and nearly double the performance of AMD’s Radeon RX 5700XT. With ray tracing and NVIDIA DLSS turned on in the game, the RTX 3080 once again crushes all previous gen NVIDIA Turing cards, and even delivers north of 60 fps performance with RTX on at 4K resolution.
If you’re looking for something a bit more mainstream, here’s HotHardware’s Red Dead Redemption 2 scores to pore over as well. The GeForce RTX 3080 delivers nearly 70 fps at 4K on the Ultra quality preset, while an RTX 2080 Super and RX 5700 XT deliver 44 and 40 fps respectively. HotHardware has a ton more benchmarks up in Marco Chiappetta’s deep-dive review, so be sure to check that out too. Comparing performance to an RTX 2080 Ti, which currently retails for around $1200 - $1300, the RTX 3080 is some 30 percent faster in this game title at this ultra-high image quality setting, for a starting MSRP of $699 for a Founder’s Edition card direct from NVIDIA. The value proposition is strong here, relatively speaking.
Finally, in terms of GPU Compute performance, the GeForce RTX 3080 with its data center machine learning Ampere DNA, is completely off the charts, offering over 2X the performance of an RTX 2080 Super in some of our testing.
Before I wrap-up my impressions of NVIDIA’s powerful new Ampere-based GeForce card, let’s talk cooling and acoustics, as well a few things to look forward to.  First, the cooling solution NVIDIA engineered for the RTX 3080 is fantastic. I appreciate the card’s performance but I’m a sucker for elegant and efficient product design. This thing just whispers under heavy load and it’s barely audible over PC desktop chassis fans. GPU temps also remained in a very modest mid-70C range under heavy benchmark workloads. An RTX 2080 Ti is actually slightly louder under load and it doesn’t exhaust nearly as much warm air out of a case like the GeForce RTX 3080 does. The new GeForce RTX 3080 is a proverbial win-win, physically speaking. It’s way more powerful but runs cooler and quieter.
Beyond what gamers are able to realize today, in terms of performance, there’s also the promise of RTX IO (details further down that page) for GeForce RTX 30 series cards, which works in conjunction with Microsoft’s DirectStorage API. RTX IO will allow game data to load from fast SSD system storage directly to GPU memory, bypassing the CPU complex for lower overhead and much faster game load times. We’re looking forward to testing a few titles that support the feature in the months ahead and — along with some of the other interesting, cool new features like NVIDIA Reflex for game input lag and latency reduction and NVIDIA Broadcast for audio and video capture quality enhancements — NVIDIA seems to have brought a lot to the table for enthusiasts with this launch, beyond just the GeForce RTX 3080 itself.
Summarizing the GeForce RTX 3080 isn’t a very tall order but, for NVIDIA’s main competitor AMD, Ampere (so far) is shaping up to be an absolute head ache and a half. I won’t make any bold predictions about how AMD’s Radeon RX 6000 Big Navi card will stack-up, but with what we’re seeing for performance so far with the GeForce RTX 3080 as the flagship GeForce RTX 30-series card first out of the gate, AMD certainly has it work cut out for it. We shall see soon enough, though if you consider NVIDIA’s GeForce RTX 3080 on its own merits versus NVIDIA’s previous generation RTX 2080 when it launched, or the RTX 2080 Ti currently, I’ll say it again, NVIDIA crushed it. Starting at $699 for a Founder’s Edition card and shipping on 9/17, gamers are going to snap these things up and they’ll sell like hotcakes. Further, with Ampere A100 in the data center already and GeForce RTX 3080, RTX 3070 and RTX 3090 hitting here in the Q4 holiday shopping season, look for big numbers for NVIDIA’s fiscal Q4 at the end of January. It’s as simple as supply and demand.
