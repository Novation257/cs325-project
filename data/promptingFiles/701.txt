Nvidia GeForce GTX 1080 Review: Hail To The King

May 17, 2016, 09:00am EDT

Somewhere along the winding road of development that stretches between Maxwell and Pascal, I feel like Nvidia decided to play by slightly different rules. They got even more ambitious, even more aggressive. On May 27, Nvidia will release the GeForce GTX 1080 graphics card and make their own GTX 900 series feel immediately and palpably irrelevant. See, the GTX 1080 isn't just an iterative improvement that makes you question whether or not the price-to-performance ratio justifies a purchase. It's one hell of a part, and an astounding generational leap forward that finds Nvidia surging way ahead in performance while offering an uncharacteristically aggressive price.
You need some proof, yes? Fortunately I've done the testing, I've done the math, and I've done the testing again because I didn't initially believe the math. So let's jump in!
Crucial GeForce GTX 1080 Specs
During its GTX 1080 reveal earlier this month, Nvidia was keen to compare their newest flagship to the Titan X, emphasizing that it's faster than their full-fat Maxwell monster for $400 less.  They weren't remotely kidding, by the way. In my testing the GTX 1080 consistently outpaces the Titan X by a significant margin. The real comparison, however, should be the GTX 980, which launched at $549. Yes, that's $50 cheaper than its successor. And yes, you'll soon realize it's definitely worth the price hike.
Nvidia also mentioned that they spent billions in R&D developing and rolling out this new Pascal architecture, and while I'm not going to go all "white paper" on you, I have to say that there's a lot of magic happening here and it's worth briefly highlighting some of the advancements, with deeper dives into features like Ansel, VR, and Simultaneous Multi-Projection down the road. Today I'm going to show you what matters most to me, and probably to you: performance.
Here, then, are some of the key GeForce GTX 1080 specs:
The 16nm FinFET process the Pascal architecture is built on means that Nvidia can cram even more transistors (7.2 billion versus 5.2 billion in the GTX 980) into a smaller space, amp up the eye candy, and do so using less power. The utilization of Micron's new GDDR5X video memory delivers 43% more memory bandwidth than the GTX 980, but architectural improvements and some new memory compression tricks translate to a total effective memory bandwidth increase of 1.7x.
And let's talk about those clock speeds! The GTX 1080 has a base clock of 1607MHz, but tops out at 1733MHz under its boost clock. Compare this to the 1216MHz boost clock of the GTX 980 and we're looking at 42% faster clock speeds. Combined with faster memory and enhanced memory compression, Nvidia's latest graphics card is more than an iterative release. It absolutely decimates its predecessor, blows right by the Titan X, and consistently topples AMD's Radeon Fury X.
Nvidia claims the GTX 1080 is the "new king," and that's more than marketing fluff. It's an impressive generational leap that's incredible on its own, but also foreshadows some of the surprisingly affordable graphics processing horsepower on the horizon with products like the GTX 1070 and GTX 1060.
Test System: The 2016 "People's Bench"
Earlier this year I reconsidered my stance on benchmarking and decided to build a system I dubbed "The People's Bench." The goal was to show you what’s realistic and attainable, not what's possible only for the upper echelon of PC gamers out there with insanely expensive CPUs. My philosophy is that the most expensive component of your gaming PC should be the graphics card, so while 
Intel&#039;s
 i7-5960x -- the CPU most tech outlets use for testing -- is an absolute monster, its price excludes it from my personal bench. And honestly, the majority of cards I'll test in the next year are going to be sub-$350 parts. So I've settled on the Core i5-6400 because it meets the requirements for VR and still delivers awesome performance for mainstream gaming.
Bearing that in mind, it's important to note that my benchmark results don't represent the highest achievable framerates with the GTX 1080. Instead, we're going to focus on the massive performance increase we're seeing when comparing it to its predecessor, the GTX 980. Or when comparing it to the $999 Titan X. Or when comparing it to AMD's $649 Fury X flagship. Or, well, just about any single-GPU consumer graphics card in existence.
All testing was done using a clean install of Windows 10 Pro, Nvidia's GeForce 368.16 driver (press only at time of publication), and AMD's Radeon Crimson 16.5.2 driver. It's also important to note that all cards in this review were retested using these drivers. Additionally, all cards are either reference cards or using baseline clock speeds. No overclocked parts to skew the results.
3DMark Firestrike
3DMark’s Fire Strike is a synthetic benchmark that tests a system’s overall graphics capabilities, but it’s not a replacement for real-world gaming results. Still, it tends to provide a fairly accurate snapshot of the competitive advantage one card can have over another. You can’t hand down a verdict based solely on this test, but it’s a solid indicator of performance. Let's see if it establishes a trend that we can track throughout all of these benchmarks at 1440p and 4K.
There's no argument from 3DMark disputing the 1080's crown. The FireStrike Extreme benchmark shows the GTX 1080 outperforming the GTX 980 by 52%, the Titan X by 17%, and AMD's Radeon Fury X by 23%. That performance uplift remains consistent at 4K with the Firestrike Ultra test.
UPDATE: I revisited Firestrike Extreme with the GTX 1080 and Intel's aforementioned 8-core i7-5960x. The resulting score was 9544, which equals about a 15% boost in performance moving from the i5-6400 to the i7-5960x. That makes sense for a monster GPU like this. Again, I want to emphasize that my results don't represent the ceiling; instead they represent a more normalized group of gamers.
That being said, if you want to pair a more powerful CPU with this card, you're not going to be disappointed.
Unigine Heaven 4.0
Unigine's Heaven Benchmark is an awesome tool that utilizes the Unigine Engine, and testing can be done with DirectX 10, DirectX 11, or OpenGL. I run the Heaven benchmark in DX11 mode which utilizes tons of tessellation and screen-space ambient occlusion. As with 3DMark's tests, it's not always indicative of real-world gaming scenarios, but it does provide that performance snapshot we need to start identifying trends.
Nvidia has always excelled at tessellation, but the GTX 1080 just soars here. I cranked every knob to Extreme or Ultra, and maxed out the anti-aliasing at 8x. Not only is Nvidia's latest way out in front of the GTX 980 (a whopping 67% faster), but it's 21% better than the Titan X. The Fury X lags behind as well, and truth be told AMD's excellent flagship will never close that gap throughout this review, even in DirectX 12 tests like Ashes of the Singularity and Rise of the Tomb Raider.
Jump to Page 2 and let's do this.
Ashes of the Singularity (DX12)
Speaking of Ashes of the Singularity, the strategy title is now out of Beta and available for public consumption. Stardock's RTS was an early flag-bearer for AMD's DirectX 12 leadership, which is now being challenged. Obviously the Fury and Fury X are both quite strong here, but the GTX 1080 still edges them out by 12% at 4K resolution.
It's also here that a pattern begins to emerge. Make no mistake, the 1080 is going to win every benchmark under the sun; it's the degree to which it's winning that is the talking point. But it seems to jump at the chance to prove itself at higher resolutions. Under the 1440p test, it's 30% faster than the 980 and only 10% faster than the Titan X. When we switch to 4K? It crushes the 980 with a 66% performance uplift and the Titan X with a 30% uplift.
Rise of the Tomb Raider (DX12)
Let's take a look at another DirectX 12 title then. Rise of the Tomb Raider is the newest Lara Croft adventure, developed by Crystal Dynamics with an excellent PC port by Nixxes.
The performance gap is...embarassing.
The chart just kind of reaches out and slaps you in the face. The Fury, Fury X, and GTX 980 are all locked at around 60fps in the high quality preset 1440p test, and the GTX 1080 shows up and trounces all of them by a minimum of 69%. Sixty. Nine. Percent.
Breaking things down at 4K, we see that the GTX 1080 is 66% faster than the GTX 980, 27% faster than the Titan X, and 48% faster than AMD's Fury X.
Middle Earth: Shadow of Mordor
Let's switch gears now to DirectX 11, which should remain relevant for several years to come. Middle Earth: Shadow of Mordor has typically been a prominent feather in AMD's cap, despite being an Nvidia-optimized title. When I reviewed the Radeon Fury X, it easily outperformed the GTX 980 and GTX 980 Ti at 4K. That being said, let's have a look at how things stack up when the GTX 1080 enters the battlefield.
It's notable that the Fury X steps on the Titan X here, as expected, but it's also notable that the GTX 1080 then beats the Fury X by 25% at 4K.
Still the king. Let's move on to GTA V.
Grand Theft Auto V
Rockstar's incredibly well optimized PC port was worth the wait, but it still requires significant horsepower to push high quality visuals at 60fps under 1440p and 4K resolutions.
Weirdly, there's not much to shout about when it comes to 1440p performance. Then again, all cards tested perform exceptionally well with framerates we can't complain about.
Under the demands of 4K, though, the GTX 1080 reasserts itself and reminds us that this is a 4K gamer's dream card, bringing home 99fps with all the bells and whistles cranked to high quality, including post-processing and ambient occlusion. This represents a 65% performance boost over the GTX 980, and a 25% boost over the once-mighty Titan X.
Far Cry Primal

 Far Cry Primal, which utilizes Dunia Engine 2 for some really lush graphics and beautifully rendered humans and animals. It definitely requires a high-end GPU to really flex its graphical polish. At this stage we know what to expect. 65% faster than the 980 in 4K? Check. 26% faster than the Titan X? Check.
The king is looking down at its competition, and the king finds them lacking. Especially considering the GTX 1080 costs only 9% more than the GTX 980 when it launched.
Less Power, More Performance
Nvidia devoted a lot of their resources to optimizing and improving power efficiency with Maxwell, and they're not slowing down on that front. The first clue is that the GTX 1080 only requires a single 8-pin PCIe connector, and boasts a TDP of 180 Watts. Compare that to the 250W TDP of the Titan X, and then consider that it's outperforming it by 30% or more.
To put it to the test, I measured total system power draw at the wall while running the demanding Firestrike Extreme benchmark. Normally we'll see the heaviest power draw during the final "Combined Test" portion. Here's how that shook out:
The GTX 1080 is using roughly the same power consumption as a GTX 980 under load, but 52% more performance as measured during the Firestrike Extreme benchmark.
2.1GHz? Let's Overclock This Thing
When Nvidia revealed the GTX 1080, they showed it running with a massive 2.1GHz overclock, on air, and operating at a cool 67C. It seemed incomprehensible, but it mostly checks out. Remember that every GPU is going to have slight variances in its ability to overclock, and my review sample actually hit 2.1GHz momentarily without crashing, although it throttled back to about 2040MHz after a couple minutes under load. As you can see, I cranked up the GPU clock by 245MHz, and the already blazing fast memory clock by 200MHz. Of course, the fan had to be cranked up to 100% and my temp topped out at 74C.
This resulted in roughly 11% - 15% better performance across several titles which can often tip the framerate scales over 60fps at 4K resolution. There's some nice headroom here giving patient overclockers plenty of free performance, but I'm eager to see what a liquid-cooled GTX 1080 variant can do.
Noise, Notes, And The Road Ahead
I wasn't able to properly evaluate the GTX 1080's noise output in a soundproof setting with a sound meter, but I did use an app that measures dB to at least get a ballpark impression. And that impression is that the GTX 1080 is just a few decibels louder than the excellent GTX 980 under load, and just a touch quieter than the Titan X. Remember, this is for Nvidia's Founder's Edition, not the upcoming partner cards from companies like PNY, Asus, MSI, and EVGA. Those cards will have coolers that need to be individually evaluated.
I'd like this GTX 1080 review to more resemble the beginning of a long and exciting conversation rather than a final verdict. There is an enormous amount of Pascal-related features to dig into still spanning stuff like memory compression, positional audio, Ansel, and virtual reality. There are still an unknown number of partner cards releasing at a suggested retail price of $599, each with unique traits, design language, and cooling solutions.
What we know so far is ridiculously compelling. The GTX 1080 boasts an average 50% better performance than the GTX 980 at 1440p across 7 synthetic and in-game benchmarks. And it rises to the 4K challenge, kicking out an average 65% higher framerates across those same tests compared to the GTX 980. It does this while sipping roughly the same amount of power as its predecessor.
All of this, and its entry price is only 9% higher than its 900 series part. A year ago this would have been a $999 graphics card.
Exciting things are on the horizon, though, and they're worth paying attention to. While the GTX 1080 is finally the single-GPU card that enables 4K/60fps gaming, the $379 GTX 1070 is said to offer Titan X-level performance. If that's the case we're going to have a fascinating conversation once those cards make it to reviewers. And if Nvidia's claims about the 1070 ring true, one can only imagine what the inevitable GTX 1060 could bring to the party. Perhaps it could rival the GTX 980, retail for about $249, and lower the price barrier to desktop-class VR experiences?
Let's not forget Polaris, with AMD's official unveiling set to occur later this month. I don't really report on rumors and speculation, but AMD itself has noted that Polaris will target a more mainstream audience as opposed to ultra-enthusiasts. It's Vega that stands a chance to unseat Nvidia's new king, but for now I'm 100% confident in recommending the GTX 1080 to anyone willing to part with the cash. It's a terrific value regardless of what's ahead.
