NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3060 Ti Debuts With Exceptional Performance Per Gaming Dollar

Dec 01, 2020, 09:01am EST

NVIDIA is launching yet another graphics card for gamers, just in time for the holiday shopping season today, and this product is aimed squarely at the meat of the market. Setting a new midrange performance bar at a $399 price point, NVIDIA’s GeForce RTX 3060 Ti has been officially unveiled, though rumors and leaks have been circulating for a few weeks now. The GeForce RTX 3060 Ti is based on the same NVIDIA Ampere GPU architecture that underpins NVIDIA’s new higher-end GeForce RTX 3070 and 3080 cards, though it’s a cut back version aimed at more value-minded gamers.
Specifically, the new GeForce RTX 3060 Ti is built on the same NVIDIA GA104 GPU, but with 4864 CUDA cores, versus a 5888 found in a higher-end RTX 3070, though that’s over twice the number of cores in a previous gen RTX 2060 Super at 2176. There are fewer tensor cores on board at 152 total (272 for an RTX 2060 Super), though those cores are up to 2X faster, according to NVIDIA. Finally, for cutting-edge ray tracing workloads in the latest game engines, the GeForce RTX 3060 Ti sports 38 2nd generation RT cores versus the 2060 Super’s 34 first gen cores, and delivers 31.6 RT TFLOPs, or about 45 percent more available RT throughput versus the company’s previous gen RTX 2060 Super card.

Core clock speeds for these extra silicon resources, however, are largely unchanged, with a 1665MHz GPU Boost Clock for the RTX 3060 Ti versus 1650MHz for an RTX 2060 Super, while both the previous gen and this new Ampere GPU share the same 7000 MHz GDDR6 Memory Clock and 8GB of GDDR6 RAM over a 256-bit memory interface.
Physically, the new Founders Edition GeForce RTX 3060 Ti looks almost completely identical to NVIDIA’s smaller footprint and efficient GeForce RTX 3070 FE card, though it is adorned in a slightly lighter, more silver colored brushed aluminum finish. It also shares the same 12-pin PCIe power connector centrally located on the top edge of the card, though 3rd party board partners will also likely offer standard 8-pin (and dual 8-pin) PCIe power connector configurations.
So that’s how the GeForce 3060 Ti stacks up in terms of the speeds and feeds, but what PC gamers really want to know is how it performs competitively at its $399 price point. Let’s take a quick gander...
In short, it’s the fastest GPU in this price range currently, sometimes by a significant margin, leaving behind AMD’s now aging Radeon RX 5700 XT virtually across the board, regardless of game title. Check out Marco’s GeForce RTX 3060 Ti review at HotHardware for lots more benchmark results. Also, as noted, the RTX 3060 Ti brings with it NVIDIA’s latest generation ray tracing engine, for greater visual fidelity with games that now support the technology, as well as a growing list of next-gen titles that showcase RT effects, like CyberPunk 2077, which is due to launch next week.
The bottom line competitively is that AMD has yet to show its hand on what a “Big Navi” Radeon 6000 Series offering could achieve in this high volume price band, and until that happens it’s game, set, and match for NVIDIA in $400 graphics cards. AMD’s latest generation competitive card currently, the Radeon RX 6800, retails (or is supposed to) for a full $179 more, though it’s significantly more powerful and closer aligned somewhere between NVIDIA’s $499 GeForce RTX 3070 and $699 GeForce RTX 3080. AMD’s Radeon RX 6800 does support ray tracing as well, but currently trails the RTX 3060 Ti generally speaking, largely because NVIDIA has been working with game developers for two generations now with its second generation of ray tracing silicon core technology.
Beyond these dynamics, the big question will be whether or not consumers will be able to realize enough quantities of these cards to meet demand in retail channels. Demand for this generation of graphics cards, for both NVIDIA and AMD, has been off the hook. However, lower cost offerings like the RTX 3060 Ti typically have better yield and better availability. In fact, it’s likely that the new GeForce RTX 3060 Ti is a solid contributor to NVIDIA’s bottom line in short order, and it will be interesting to check in the company’s calendar Q4 financials early next year.
