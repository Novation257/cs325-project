Nvidia RTX 4060 Ti 8GB Vs AMD Radeon RX 6750 XT And RTX 3060 Ti: Which Should You Buy?

May 23, 2023, 09:00am EDT

Nvidia has today allowed reviewers of its new GeForce RTX 4060 Ti graphics card to reveal just how fast it is in games, ahead of making the card available on 24th May. The new model, which has 8GB of video memory, will be followed by a 16GB variant in May as well as a cheaper RTX 4060. Today we're focussing on the RTX 4060 Ti 8GB and how it compared to other graphics cards out there, including the AMD Radeon RX 6750 XT and Nvidia's older RTX 3060 Ti.

Pricing is critical for Nvidia and it already faces stiff competition from AMD's Radeon RX 6750 XT, which has 12GB of video memory compared to 8GB for the Nvidia card, and has recently received a price cut with many models available for below $380, while the RTX 4060 Ti 8GB will start at $399.
However, today's launch should also see pricing of the RTX 3060 Ti fall, with its price remaining stubbornly high with few models available for under $400. Clearly the graphics card we're looking at today should be faster, so there's definitely pressure there for Nvidia to keep pricing keen, both with the RTX 4060 Ti 8GB and its predecessor.
It's significantly trimmed in terms of specifications compared to the RTX 4070, with noticeably fewer Cuda cores, Tensor cores and RT cores as well as a slimmer 128-bit memory bus compared to 192-bit for the RTX 4070, which also has 12GB of video memory and a larger 36MB cache compared to 32MB for the RTX 4060 Ti.
I used an Intel Core i9-12900K, 32GB of Kingston Fury 6400MHz DDR5 memory strapped to an NZXT N7 Z790 motherboard along with the latest drivers and Windows 11 updates. As the RTX 4060 Ti is primarily a mid-range model facing 1080p and 1440p resolutions, I've focussed on these in the graphs but have included 4K performance with DLSS enabled on occasions to show its benefits, especially as DLSS 3.0 is supported. I've used the latest drivers and Windows 11 updates and BAR/Smart Access Memory is enabled. I've carried out 1080 testing on a select number of cards, but where possible, included results for high-end models for comparison at higher resolutions.
Benchmarks
Below we can see the RTX 4060 Ti 8GB doing well in Halo Infinite at 1080p, even outstripping the RX 6800 XT, but this is one game that has proven to have an Nvidia bias. The same was true at 1440p, and the minimum 99th percentile frame rate was particularly impressive, beating much higher-end cards, even if those managed much higher average frame rates.
In Rainbow Six Extraction, the RX 6750 XT proved to be slightly faster at both 1080p and 1440p while the RTX 4060 Ti did offer decent gains over the RTX 3060 Ti.
Below in Flight Simulator, my original benchmark has more of a bottleneck elsewhere in the system as so there's not a huge amount of variation at 1080p. Stepping up to 1440p and some of the higher end models pull away, while the RTX 4060 Ti is left battling with the RX 6750 XT.
In Watch Dogs: Legion, the RTX 4060 Ti was able to outstrip the RTX 3060 Ti and RX 6750 XT by big margins but it was bettered by the RTX 3070. It was still a strong card at 1440p, nearly matching the RX 6800 XT and offering bigger gains over the RTX 3060 Ti and RX 6750 XT.
Far Cry 6 below sees the RX 6750 XT once again keep pace with the RTX 4060 Ti, although the latter did beat the RTX 3070 and RTX 3060 Ti convincingly at 1080p, with a similar story playing out at 1440p.
Below in Forza Horizon 5, the RTX 4060 Ti was able to match the RTX 3070 and offer decent gains over the RTX 3060 Ti, but the RX 6750 XT was noticeably quicker at 1080p while less so at 1440p.
Metro Exodus proved to be a decent result for Nvidia, with the RTX 4060 Ti offering much more performance than the RX 6750 XT while the older RTX 3060 Ti was much slower than the new card.
The RTX 4060 Ti is the cheapest card Nvidia has that supports DLSS 3.0 and the company makes a big deal out of this frame rate-boosting tech being a prized feature so checking out DLSS performance in at least a few games is important.
In Metro Exodus, DLSS enables the RTX 4060 Ti to offer comfortable frame rates at 4K and by comparison the RX 6750 XT could only manage an average frame rate of 22fps here compared to 62fps. Again, AMD has its own version of DLSS in FSR, but in games that only suppose DLSS such as this, Nvidia definitely has an advantage.
Below in Flight Simulator we see DLSS 3.0 and frame generation in action and here the RTX 4060 Ti was able to match an RTX 4090 and RX 7900 XT. With DLSS 3.0 and FSR enabled, those cards were much faster, but it goes to show that if you're prepared to use it, DLSS 3.0 can be very beneficial, even more so when it comes to dealing with bottlenecks elsewhere in the system.
Rainbow Six Extraction also saw silky-smooth frame rates at 4K with the RTX 4060 Ti again able to keep pace with the AMD RX 7900 XT, although not offering a huge amount more performance than the RTX 3060 Ti.
By far the best result here is the power consumption. The RTX 4060 Ti system as a whole used just 273W, which was nearly 100W less than the AMD RX 6750 XT and around 60W less than the RTX 3060 Ti. This makes it especially appealing to anyone that appreciates efficiency as well as low temperatures and the RTX 4060 Ti is a perfect card small form factor PCs.
Seeing an RTX 40-series card for less than $500 has been a long time coming, although it remains to be seen just how pricy the RTX 4060 Ti will be when it lands on shelves tomorrow. It needs to stick close to its target of $399 given AMD's RX 6750 XT was as fast or faster in quite a few areas despite costing less, but Nvidia can't really be accused of inflating prices this time as the RTX 3060 Ti cost the same at launch as did the RTX 2060 Super.
Power efficiency is absolutely incredible, so if you don't want to upgrade your cooling, case or power supply, the RTX 4060 Ti is ideal. In fact, you'll likely see a reduction in your electricity bill given it draws far less power even than the RTX 3060 Ti and RX 6750 XT.
In terms of gaming performance, the picture is, as usual, muddied by DLSS. In rasterization, the AMD RX 6750 XT is often a match and costs less, but there are plenty of situations where it's a lot slower here too. Clearly, though, if you can pick up an RX 6750 XT for less than $350, that's going some way to justifying those slower results. It's also true that if you own an RTX 3070, the RTX 4060 Ti isn't much of an upgrade either. Add in DLSS, though, and overall the RTX 4060 Ti makes a very strong case for itself that's also extremely power frugal. Whether spending an extra $100 and waiting for the 16GB model arriving in July is worth it, remains to be seen.
