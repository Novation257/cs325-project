AMD Radeon RX 6600 Vs Nvidia RTX 3060: Which Should You Buy?

Oct 13, 2021, 09:00am EDT

Today AMD (NASDAQ: AMD) launches the new Radeon RX 6600 graphics card - the sixth installment of its RDNA 2-based Radeon 6000-series products - and we can see just how fast it is in the latest games compared to the Nvidia's RTX 3060 and also AMD's recently released RX 6600 XT too.
The RX 6600 is primarily focussed on 1080p gaming so that's where I'll be aiming my benchmarks. As usual, pricing will vary hugely depending on where you live, with the microchip shortage continuing to play havoc with global supplies of graphics cards. Again, as usual, you'll need to get the best card you can afford in your area and use these results to pick between them.
Above we can see the RX 6600 is quite cut back from the RX 6600 XT, with four fewer compute units, a hefty climb down in game frequency and also stream processors. However, both feature 8GB GDDR6 memory, a 16Gbps memory speed and 128-bit memory interface, which should land the RX 6600 into solid 1080p gaming performance territory, albeit some way down the graphs from its sibling.
A small extra test I've carried out today is AMD's FidelityFX Super Resolution. Akin to Nvidia's DLSS, this upscaling, performance-boosting technology only works in a handful of games and today I'll be trying it in the new Far Cry 6 to see how much of a boost you'll see if you enable it. Of course, you'll have to make up your own mind as to how the image quality compares as there are some polarising opinions out there.
Similarly, where possible, I've also enabled Nvidia's DLSS and ray tracing too via DirectX Raytracing (DXR).
My hardware includes a water-cooled BarrowCH Rhopilema water-cooled test bench, 16GB Corsair Vengeance RGB Pro 3,466MHz memory, Intel Core i9-11900K, an Intel 760p M.2 SSD, Corsair RM850i PSU and MSI MEG Z590 Ace motherboard. I've carried out all tests with resizable BAR/Smart Access Memory (SAM) support enabled where possible.
First up is Far Cry 6 and without DXR enabled, the RX 6600 sat directly behind the RTX 3060 with just a few frames separating the two. However, with DLSS not available in this game, AMD has the same advantage Nvidia has enjoyed, with FidelityFX Super Resolution seeing a massive boost to frame rates and the RX 6600 XT matching the RX 6700 XT.
With DXR enabled, FidelityFX gives it an even better advantage, bettering both the RX 6700 XT and RTX 3060, which jumped up the graph once ray tracing was enabled. Clearly where it's an option and if you're otherwise happy to enable it, FidelityFX is definitely worse using. Without it, the RX 6600 sits where you'd expect it to - just behind the RX 6600 XT.
For some reason the RX 6600 struggled in Flight Simulator, falling 10fps on the 99th percentile behind the RX 6600 XT. AMD is usually quite strong here, with the RX 6700 XT matching or battering everything Nvidia has below the RTX 3070, but if you need those landings and takeoffs to be as smooth as possible, it could well be worth opting for the RTX 3060 or RX 6600 XT instead.
The new enhanced edition of Metro Exodus generally sees higher average frame rates on Nvidia cards and the same was true with the RX 6600 XT. It managed a 30fps 99th percentile compared to the RTX 3060's 33fps, but was well down on the average at 44fps to 55fps, and also noticeably slower than the RX 6600 XT.
Another AMD-favoring game, Dirt 5 gave the RX 6600 a much easier ride, matching the RTX 3060, but sitting a good drop below the RX 6600 XT.
Above is Watch Dogs: Legion with DXR enabled and once again, we saw driver bugs with both the RX 6600 XT and RX 6600 seeing pitiful frame rates. Strangely, this didn't happen with the RX 6700 XT even with the same driver so it's clearly an issue that rears its head with those combinations and not all AMD cards.
Disabling DXR sees frame rates return to normal and here the RX 6600 cam close to matching the RTX 3060 and was 10 percent slower on the minimum 99th percentile than the RX 6600 XT. FidelityFX isn't supported here but DLSS is, given Nvidia a big advantage if you're able and willing to use it.
Finally we have Wolfenstein and even at Uber settings, 1080p was no problem for the RX 6600, although it was a long way from matching the RTX 3060 and again quite well below the RX 6600 XT.
As expected the RX 6600 was the most power-frugal card on test, undercutting the RX 6600 XT and allowing most manufacturers of the card to use comparatively tiny coolers. AMD's 450W power supply recommendation is also 100W lower than Nvidia's for the RTX 3060, although you'll still need a PSU with an 8-pin power connector for all RX 6600's.
There are a few situations where both the Nvidia RTX 3060 and AMD RX 6600 XT are noticeably faster than the RX 6600. Enabling DirectX Raytracing (DXR) in Far Cry 6, for example, gives the RTX 3060 a significant lead, while the RX 6600 XT was much faster in Microsoft Flight Simulator and Wolfenstein: Youngblood, perhaps more than the price difference would suggest. Watch Dogs: Legion still seems to exhibit drastic slow downs when DXR is enabled too, just like the RX 6600 XT, while testing with the same driver with the RX 6700 XT saw normal frame rates.
However, there are plenty of places it keeps pace with both the RTX 3060 and RX 6600 XT. For example, in Far Cry 6 without DXR, the RX 6600 was just a few frames behind the RTX 3060 and level-pegging with it in Dirt 5 with ray tracing enabled. It was also pretty close to matching the Nvidia card in Watch Dogs: Legion.
The stand-out results, though, are with AMD's FidelityFX Super Resolution. This is the first review I've done using this new feature and finally, AMD has something to stand up to Nvidia's DLSS and in terms of boosting frame rates through upscaling and even at 1080p I saw some massive increases, even in balanced mode. The RX 6600 leapfrogged the RTX 3060 in Far Cry 6 with DXR enabled and closely matched the RX 6700 XT when DXR was disabled.
Of course, the real issue is that AMD is a couple of years behind Nvidia in rolling this out and the green team has just ticked over with 120 DLSS-enabled titles, with AMD only offering FidelityFX Super Resolution support in a fraction of that number. The other issue here, though, is availability and pricing. The RX 6600 XT is currently retailing for around $700 - that's double its launch price, so it's clear that things are far from normal still and it's almost impossible to recommend one card over another as you simply can't buy anything anywhere near its launch price right now.
