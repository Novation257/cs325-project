NVIDIA’s Next-Generation Ampere-Based GeForces Will Likely Offer Killer Performance

Jul 31, 2020, 04:10pm EDT

The rumor mill has been steadily speculating on NVIDIA’s next-generation consumer GPUs, based on the company’s Ampere architecture, for quite a while now. In fact, I personally had multiple, off-the-record conversations regarding Ampere with a number of NVIDIA’s board partners way back during CES in early January, and all of them had good things to say.
As we inch closer to the GeForce RTX 3000 series’ eventual release (assuming that is what NVIDIA actually calls it), however, more and more leaks are hitting the web and virtually all of them suggest that the new cards are going to offer some killer performance.
All signs at this point suggest a September launch for NVIDIA’s next-gen GeForces, which is two years exactly since the GeForce RTX 2000 series launched in September 2018. Considering the Turing-based GeForce RTX 2000 series was NVIDIA’s first consumer GPU to feature Tensor and RTX cores (Volta was never a consumer GPU), NVIDIA has likely found a number of ways to optimize their performance and efficiency with consumer and gaming workloads. When talking about its data center / HPC focused Ampere A100 GPU, NVIDIA says, “The new third-generation Tensor Core architecture in A100 delivers double the raw dense Tensor throughput per SM compared to V100, accelerates more data types, and delivers tremendous additional 2x speedups for sparse matrix computations.” In addition to enhancements made to the cores themselves, Ampere also features larger caches, more total cores, wider interfaces, support for new data compression types, and additional enhancements throughout the GPU. All of that should culminate in significantly increased performance all around.
In some officially released ML Perf benchmarks that hit the web yesterday, NVIDIA announced record breaking AI performance on Ampere, that’s up to 4X higher than Volta. And a number of recent leaks suggest that the flagship Ampere-based GeForce RTX 3000 series card – likely to be branded the GeForce RTX 3080 Ti – will be approximately 50% faster than the currently available GeForce RTX 2080 Ti with gaming and rendering workloads.
A couple of days ago, Jules Urbach, CEO and co-founder of OTOY Tweeted, “The NVIDIA A100 has now become the fastest GPU ever recorded on #OctaneBench: 446 OB4* #Ampere appears to be ~43 percent faster than #Turing in #OctaneRender - even w/ #RTX off!” Now, that test was run on the A100 (not an upcoming consumer GPU), but it still points to massive performance improvements over existing cards, due to the architectural similarities that will exist between them.
A supposed leak of a UL 3DMark TimeSpy benchmark that hit a couple of weeks back suggested a 30% advantage for the ‘3080 Ti’ versus a GeForce RTX 2080 Ti FE card, with scores of 18,257 vs. 13,939, respectively. Another leak that hit today though, using the more taxing Time Spy Extreme benchmark, which places a heavier load on the GPU, suggests a 50% advantage for the alleged ‘3080 Ti’. A tweet from a well-known leaker suggests a Time Spy Extreme score of nearly 10,000 for the ‘3080 Ti’, whereas the average score for a single 2080 Ti in 3DMark’s database for that test is “only” 6,471 – and that includes some heavily overclocked submissions.
Of course, this is all speculation until cards actually arrive, but all signs point to big performance gains at this point. We should know for sure in just a few more weeks.
