AMD Radeon RX Vega 56 Versus Nvidia GeForce GTX 1070: Who Wins The $400 Crown?

Aug 14, 2017, 09:01am EDT

We've been waiting for what feels like an age for AMD to return to the high-end of graphics cards competition. Its Radeon RX 580 was only really aimed at dealing with Nvidia's GeForce GTX 1060 and even then, cryptocurrency mining has meant that soon after its launch, the RX 580 was nearly impossible to buy. It's over a year since Nvidia released its GTX 1070 and 1080, so the green team has enjoyed a huge amount of time at the top, unchallenged. Today, though, AMD is finally offering some competition with two new high-end cards - Radeon RX Vega 64 and Vega 56.
See my Radeon RX Vega 64 review here.
I'll be looking at each card in a separate review, pitching them against the competition. That comes in the form of the GTX 1070 for the Vega 56, and the GTX 1080 for the Vega 64. AMD has specifically stated that the Vega 64 is not a GTX 1080 Ti beater, which is perhaps a little bit of a shame, but then again, for ultra high-end systems, Nvidia only supports two-way SLI, whereas AMD's CrossFire still offers support for up to three cards.
In addition, AMD's X399 platform and Threadripper CPUs (see my review of them here), offer 64 PCI-E lanes, meaning if you do gun for a three-way CrossFire setup with a trio of Vega GPUs, this will likely outstrip a pair of GTX 1080 Tis and for the same cash. I'm digressing on a very niche system of course, but for now Nvidia is likely to retain its crown for single card performance.
So can AMD actually win here? One would hope that the Vega 56 is actually faster than a GTX 1070 given the latter has had some extended shelf time and AMD has been beavering away on Vega 10 core, which is present in both its new high-end cards. An equal footing might be a tad disappointing too, but it's worth remembering that the GTX 1070 still costs at least $450, which is $50 more than the Radeon RX Vega 56. Across the pond in the UK, AMD may have a tougher time; there are plenty of GTX 1070's for much less than £400, so AMD has a slightly different price point to consider.
It does have one secret weapon though - FreeSync, which is AMD's take on adaptive synchronization (Nvidia's is G-Sync). FreeSync monitors are cheaper and more readily available than their G-Sync counterparts, so AMD graphics cards are already a more attractive proposition for hardcore gamers in this regard. The downside, of course, is that until today, AMD was lacking in performance to achieve the high frame rates necessary for FreeSync to work well in modern games. Even if Vega isn't massively superior to Nvidia's offerings, there's still the FreeSync ecosystem to consider. Even if Vega isn't massively superior to Nvidia's offerings, there's still the FreeSync ecosystem to consider. This is further bolstered by the recent release of Enhanced Sync, which is driver-based and serves to improve FreeSync and also offer a better experience on non-FreeSync monitors than VSync. AMD also claims to have been working on improving minimum frame rates so performance sits more within typical FreeSync zones than Nvidia's alternatives.
So what's new with Vega? Both cards launching today are AMD's first implementation of its scalable GPU architecture and both feature its HBM2 memory, which is 3.5 times more efficient than GDDR5 and 75% smaller. Both the Vega 64 and Vega 56 sport 64 compute units and a 14nm manufacturing process as well as 8GB of HBM2 memory. All in all, it's the biggest change to AMD's GPU architecture in five years.

Pricing is somewhat complicated once you factor in the various discount packs that AMD offers. The basic reference models of the Vega 64 and Vega 56 retail for $399 and $499 respectively. However, there are three discount packs available too with each providing $200 off a Samsung FreeSync monitor, $100 off a CPU and motherboard combo and two free games.
AMD is offering discount packs including FreeSync Enabled Monitors, motherboards, Ryzen 7 CPUs and games. These are currently the only way to obtain Limited Edition and Liquid Cooled Vega cards for $699, you get the Aqua Pack, which offers the same discounts plus a liquid-cooled Vega 64. For $599 you get the same discounts but a limited edition Vega 64 card, so there are actually three models of Vega 64. Only one is currently available as a stand alone product.
Like Nvidia's GTX 1070 and 1080, both the RX Vega 64 and RX Vega 56 have the same amount of memory and transistors, but the former has higher core and memory frequencies and more stream processors, with the liquid cooled Vega 64 sporting even higher core frequencies. It's fairly clear that Nvidia still enjoys an advantage in the efficiency stakes, though, with the GTX 1070 only requiring a single 8-pin power connectors, with reference models having a TDP of just 150W compared to 210W for the RX Vega 56. The air-cooled cards use a new isothermic vapor chamber, 30mm blower-style fan and sport three DisplayPorts and one HDMI port.
Performance
I've benchmarked the Radeon RX Vega 56 against a Zotac GeForce GTX 1070 AMP! Edition in a variety of games and benchmarks at 1920 x 1080, 2560 x 1440 and 3840 x 2160 resolutions and will be publishing a later article including results for the Radeon RX Vega 64 and GeForce GTX 1080 as well. It's worth noting that this is a retail card, but has a modest factory overclock, so a reference GTX 1080 would actually perform slower. With Fallout 4, I edit the config file to disable Vsync and allow an unlimited frame rate. I do the same with World of Tanks. Ashes of the Singularity, Deus Ex, 3DMark VR Mark and Unigine Superposition are all carried out using built-in benchmarks. Fallout 4 and The Witcher 3 are a manual play throughs in visually demanding scenes, while World of Tanks uses the games recording function to play through a replay. Nvidia results were obtained using driver 384.94. AMD stock speed results were obtained using driver 17.30.1051 B4 and overclocked results using the B6a version of that driver.

Overclocking, noise and temperatures
The original beta 4 version of the 17.03.1051 driver AMD provided proved to have a few issues when it came to overclocking. Tweaking took an age for the simple reason the Vega 56 was happy for me to push the overclocking sliders in its Radeon Wattman software all the way to their maximums, which is quite rare. I was obviously stability testing all the way, but the same happened with the Vega 64 too. However, results were all over the place - some results once overclocked were actually slower too. Thankfully, the beta 6a driver fixed this and sadly the two cards weren't the overclocking daemons I first thought them to be. However, while the core speed no longer stating an untruthful and somewhat godlike 1900MHz+, I still managed to get the boost speed up to 1720MHz, which is close to 250MHz higher than the stock boost frequency. The memory meanwhile was happy to sit at 950MHz - a 150MHz boost over the stock speed. You can see the benefits of this below
Temperatures were on the high side and overclocking saw them reach AMD's 85°C  limit, even with the fan speed increased to an ear-splitting 4000rpm. With temperatures at stock speed already passing 75°C  after 5-10 minutes, it's clear that to avoid any throttling and to have a quieter experience, I'd strongly advise waiting for third part cooler models to arrive or indeed using some DIY liquid cooling - this card is crying out for some better cooling and I'm hopeful the likes of Asus and MSI will offer up some better cooling options. At low loads the cooler is actually fairly quiet, but becomes fairly conspicuous as soon as you jump into a game.
Conclusions
The Radeon RX Vega 56 and indeed the GeForce GTX 1070 are likely to be used for a variety of resolutions, with high refresh rate fans or those that simply prefer extremely high frame rates even dipping down to 1920 x 1080. Each card takes various top spots, but in many games, especially at the higher the resolutions, the AMD card fared better.
The GTX 1070 was quicker at 1920 x 1080 in Ashes of the Singularity, but it was the RX Vega 56 that came top at 2560 x 1440 and 3840 x 2160. Deus Ex preferred the AMD card across the board, with some sizeable wins against Nvidia hear. Fallout 4 was another game to prefer Vega at higher resolutions, with Nvidia getting beaten at 2560 x 1440 and 3840 x 2160. The Witcher 3 favored Nvidia, although there wasn't much in it at any of the resolutions, while World of Tanks was also a close call with the exception of 1920 x 1080 where there was a slightly larger lead. Power consumption isn't in AMD's favor, with the RX Vega 56 drawing an extra 70W or so under full load. Overclocking definitely yielded some benefits with an 8% increase in the minimum frame rate in Deus Ex and 7% in Fallout 4.
So, overall it's a resounding win for AMD here in the Radeon RX Vega 56 versus GeForce GTX 1070. There are a couple of instances where the latter is quicker, but where it is, it's rarely by large margins, whereas there are several instances where the AMD card is a long way ahead. On the downside, power consumption is higher, as are thermals so if I were buying Vega, I'd definitely opt for a third party-cooled model with a beefier cooler or dip my toes in liquid cooling for lower noise and cooler temperatures.
  AMD has another plus point, though, which is FreeSync. Vega now means that higher frame rates are now possible here and that's a huge boon for enthusiasts that like to take advantage of adaptive synchronization monitors. While stock is still thin to nonexistent, the RX Vega 56 is slated to be cheaper and faster too, at least in the US than the GeForce GTX 1070. As a result, if it does indeed hit the shelves at $399 the RX Vega 56 is absolutely where your cash should be going. Elsewhere, particularly in the UK, AMD will have to watch the price of some of the cheaper GTX 1070s, as some have fallen to just £329 and still sport dual-fan coolers. Partner cards should be landing in a few weeks so if I was in the market for a $400 graphics card, I'd definitely wait till then in the hope of better, quieter coolers and perhaps lower prices too.
