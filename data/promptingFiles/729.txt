Crunching The Numbers: Should You Buy An AMD R9 290X Or Nvidia GTX 780?

Oct 28, 2013, 05:24pm EDT

Last night I began the draft for a glowing review of AMD's new Radeon R9 290x graphics card. This morning Nvidia completely changed the game -- and subsequently my ultimate recommendation -- with an aggressive price drop on their GTX 780 and GTX 770 cards.
So instead of a traditional "review," I'm opting for an unbiased presentation of the benchmarks I conducted, alongside the merits of each GPU. Price points aside, Team Red and Team Green both offer compelling reasons to join their camp.
Before we dive in let's take a look at our testing environment. The system used to benchmark all three GPUs packs an 
Intel
 Core i5-4670K at 3.4GHz, an ASUS Maximus VI Gene, and 8GB of Corsair Vengeance Pro memory clocked at 1600MHz. Windows 8 handled OS duties, and the drivers used were 
Nvidia's
 331.65 WQHL and AMD's Catalyst 13.11 BetaV5 (necessary for the system to recognize the R9 290X).
Unlike the midrange 270x and 280x, AMD boasts completely new silicon in their 290x GPU. In my interview earlier this month with AMD's Matt Skynner, I was told that "this GPU is in 28nm. Some have speculated that it was 20nm and it’s not for a specific reason: At 28nm for an enthusiast GPU, we can achieve higher clock speeds and higher absolute performance." This wasn't just PR blowing hot air: In most cases AMD's new silicon outperforms its closest competitor, Nvidia's GTX 780.
In some cases it even outperforms the big daddy of single-core GPUs, Nvidia's Titan.
3DMark's FireStrike benchmark measures your GPU's performance in a battery of tests including particles, physics, CPU and GPU stress testing, tessellation, and much more. What we see in the results above is the beginning of a trend: AMD's 290x slightly outperforms Nvidia's 780 at standard 1080p resolution, but when we crank it up to 1440p (2560x1440), we see the the 290x threatening even the $999 Titan for superiority. AMD's marketing materials are hyper-focused on high resolution and UltraHD gaming, and these results start to back up that claim.
When we turn our attention to Unigine's Heaven benchmark, a slightly different picture is painted. Heaven strictly measures a graphics card's DirectX 11 performance and throws out reliance on CPU and memory. Here we see AMD dipping below both the 780 and Titan at both 1080p and 1440p. The differences are marginal but noteworthy, especially in light of Mantle and AMD's continued bashing of DirectX 11.
Synthetic benchmarks are one thing; real in-game experiences are another. To that end I've tested several games at both 1080p and 1440p. Some of these titles are optimized for Nvidia cards, some for AMD cards, and some for neither. The end goal was to identify if one card consistently dominated the other in a wide variety of games.
Right out of the gate, the 290x wanted to assert its superiority even with Batman: Arkham City, an Nvidia optimized title. Proving it wasn't a fluke, the 290x gained a 10fps lead over the 780 with Metro: Last Light, another Nvidia optimized title. Notice that it beats both the 780 and the more expensive Titan in this test. The handwriting was beginning to appear on the wall. Interestingly, Nvidia went neck-and-neck with AMD for my Tomb Raider test (yep, both benchmarks used Ultimate and TressFX). But the 290x continued to show its dominance with games like BioShock Infinite and Dirt Showdown.
Some would argue that testing these games at 1080p is pointless as these enthusiast cards are aimed squarely at gamers with 1440p or higher monitor setups. It's a fair argument, but until a single card can produce 60+fps on Metro: Last Light at 1080p with maximum settings enabled, I'll continue to test at 1080p.
That being said, let's dig into the 1440p results.
Here we see the 780 and 290x jockeying back and forth for position. AMD takes the lead in Batman: Arkham City, Metro: Last Light (again toppling even the Titan), BioShock Infinite, and distances itself with Dirt Showdown. Nvidia takes the crown for the remaining games, and it becomes evident that the 290x doesn't have the same level of dominance at 1440p, though is still plenty respectable.

The Titan offers a marginal improvement in performance, but again the 290x is practically deadlocked with the GTX 780, and this seems to be the recurring trend the more games I test. (Sadly I don't have a 4K monitor at my disposal, but I hope to revisit these benchmarks should one materialize.)
24 hours ago these results would have led to a dramatically different conclusion: Buy AMD's 290x since it's $100 cheaper and offers comparable, if not superior, performance to its closest competitor. With this morning's aggressive price cut, however, Nvidia may be causing droves of tech journalists to revisit their 290x reviews.
As things stand now, Nvidia's GTX 780 is $499, while AMD's R9 290x is $549. Make no mistake: both cards are a steal at these pricepoints. So let's talk about added value such as bundled games.
At the time of publication, most retailers and e-tailers are bundling Battlefield 4 with the 290x. I clarified with AMD PR that the company's famous "Radeon Rewards" program is only in affect for the Radeon 7000 series cards, and not for the newer R9 series. I believe that after tonight when DICE's highly anticipated shooter launches, the perceived value of Battlefield 4's inclusion will be diminished -- most enthusiasts and core shooter fans will already have the game.
In the Nvidia camp we have the GeForce Holiday Bundle which nets you free copies of Batman: Arkham Origins, Assassin's Creed IV, and Splinter Cell: Blacklist. This bundle also entitles buyers to $100 off of Nvidia's SHIELD console (a device that demands a second look following today's announcement). 3 games and a potential savings on SHIELD hardware versus one game means the raw win in terms of value goes to Nvidia.
Speaking of GeForce, Nvidia's GeForce Experience software -- now included as part of a driver download -- attempts to lower the barrier to entry by auto-configuring optimum graphics settings for the games you currently have installed. Shadowplay is a new addition to this software, offering low impact gameplay recording. These recordings happen in the background or can be toggled manually, and have almost no impact on framerate. Former FRAPS users are advised to give this a serious look.
Freebies are only part of the equation, however. Nvidia has consistently delivered better drivers at a more frequent clip, though AMD has seriously upped their game as of late. AMD also has the new Mantle API which sounds ridiculously promising, but it's tough to render a verdict until games utilizing the technology have been released.
There are considerations to bear in mind with regards to the hardware itself. Nvidia's GTX 780 is typically quieter and less power hungry than AMD's 290X, but I don't consider either of these a dealbreaker on their own.
Right now, Nvidia has the upper hand when taking all of these factors into account. But what happens if AMD returns fire and drops the 290x to $499? What if they suddenly include the 290x in their Radeon Rewards Gold bundle? Then, it may come down to simple allegiance, impulse, and sales.
Whichever you decide to purchase, feel confident that you're getting one heck of a deal for a graphics card built to withstand years of AAA game releases. But exercise patience: As we've seen today, pricing can change dramatically in a seriously short timeframe.
