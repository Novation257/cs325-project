NVIDIA Unveils GeForce RTX 30 Series Laptops And The Aggressively-Priced GeForce RTX 3060 Desktop GPU At CES

Jan 12, 2021, 12:35pm EST

CES 2021 is in full virtual swing, and major players in the lucrative gaming market are showcasing new technologies for the coming year. PC Graphics giant, NVIDIA today has taken the wraps off new GeForce RTX 30 Series gaming laptop platform technologies, as well as a new GeForce RTX 3060 graphics card that will address the meat of the market at a popular $329 midrange price band. Both product offerings are powered by the company’s latest Ampere GPU architecture that is optimized to deliver the latest cutting-edge visuals in PC gaming, like ray tracing, as well as NVIDIA’s own DLSS (Deep Learning Super Sampling) technology that improves visual fidelity while increasing performance dramatically.
In partnership with leading laptop OEMs today, NVIDIA announced new GeForce RTX 3080, RTX 3070 and RTX 3060-powered gaming laptops, starting at $999. Along with these new Ampere-based mobile GPUs, NVIDIA is highlighting its 3rd Gen Max-Q power and performance optimization technology that utilizes AI neural networks to load balance power requirements between both the CPU and GPU on a frame-by-frame basis for optimal performance. AI algorithms also drive GeForce RTX 30-series laptops for NVIDIA’s Whisper 2.0 technology that manages GPU and CPU fan speeds, and system temperatures, to optimize performance and acoustics.
These new GeForce RTX 30-series laptops will also support NVIDIA’s DLSS 2.0 technology like all Ampere GPU-based products, as well as Resizable BAR, which is a technology first introduced by AMD earlier this year, though it’s a standard PCI Express feature that’s been around for a while. Resizable BAR allows the entirety of the on-board GPU memory to be accessed directly by the CPU at once, saving costly PCI Express transaction overhead, and boosting performance in games.
DLSS 2.0 or Deep Learning Super Sampling especially is a boon for laptop performance. The technology again employs AI algorithms and on-board GeForce RTX Tensor cores to drive a significant performance lift at similar power consumption. The technology is especially useful at 1440p and higher resolutions, where only a fraction of the pixels (at a lower resolution) are required to render a frame in a game engine, while AI algorithms complete the frame, maintaining high visual fidelity but increasing performance dramatically.

NVIDIA is also claiming that over half of the GeForce RTX 30 series laptops on the market will offer 240Hz or higher refresh rate displays, for eSports gamers in need of ultra-fast twitch responsiveness. However, it’s likely that gamers will need a GeForce RTX 3080-class GPU to drive this high of a frame rate at 1080p. GeForce RTX 30 Series Studio laptops were also announced, optimized for content creators looking to drive GPU-accelerated video and other rendering workloads at high resolutions.
NVIDIA notes that GeForce RTX 30-series laptops will arrive in market starting January 26th from major PC OEMs like Acer, Dell-Alienware, ASUS, Gigabyte, HP, Lenovo, MSI and Razer. They will also be available from boutique builders, including CyberPower PC, Digital Storm, Falcon NW, Maingear, Origin PC, and XOTIC PC.
And of course, not to leave desktop PC gamers for want this CES, NVIDIA announced a new lower cost GeForce RTX 3060 GPU today. Like the aforementioned GeForce RTX 30 Series laptops, new GeForce RTX 3060 desktop cards will support the entire complement of NVIDIA’s Ampere GPU architecture, including ray tracing, DLSS, NVIDIA Reflex latency reduction technology and NVIDIA Broadcast video and audio streaming/production technologies.
NVIDIA also states that the GeForce RTX 3060 will bring twice the raster performance and 10X the ray tracing performance of its previous generation GeForce GTX 1060 card (though GTX cards don’t officially support RT in hardware). The GeForce RTX 3060 is an interesting mix of specifications, with about 20% less compute horsepower versus a GeForce RTX 3060 Ti, but with 4GB more GDDR 6 memory on board, though over a narrower 192-bit memory interface (versus 256-bit for an RTX 3060 Ti).
That extra memory is a good value for gamers at this price point actually, and I’d expect the RTX 3060 to land somewhere in between a GeForce RTX 2060 and RTX 2070 in terms of raster performance, but offer stronger ray tracing performance than both previous gen RTX 20 series cards. We shall see, once GPUs hit the market in February.
I’d expect new GeForce RTX 30 Series laptops to make a solid impact in the market this year, as gaming notebooks are a growing and popular product segment with a passionate following. New GeForce RTX 3060 cards from various 3rd party partners should also move well, though that will be contingent on NVIDIA and its partners getting past currently frustrating shortage issues spurred by intense demand and a renewed crypto-mining gold rush.
