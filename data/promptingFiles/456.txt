Nvidia’s GeForce RTX 4060 Ti Brings Advanced Gaming To Mainstream Price Points

May 23, 2023, 11:14am EDT

The PC gaming market is big business these days, and hard core gaming enthusiasts love burly, flagship graphics cards that are frankly too expensive for most mainstream consumers. As such, gaming GPUs (Graphics Processing Units) at more palatable $400 and under price bands are where the volume unit sales are. And so with each generation, Nvidia and its rival competitors typically launch mainstream optimized versions of their halo high-end products, in an effort to mop up the higher unit volume mainstream opportunity, and this is exactly where Nvidia’s new GeForce RTX 4060 series is targeted. At $399 for an 8GB VRAM Founders Edition version, GeForce RTX 4060 Ti cards will arrive in market this week, with $499 16GB and $299 standard RTX 4060 8GB variants arriving in July this Summer.
The first aspect of Nvidia’s new GeForce RRTX 4060 Ti that stands-out most for me is its power efficiency. Despite having more compute resources and effective memory bandwidth, the new GeForce RTX 4060 Ti offers more with less power consumption and a 160 Watt TGP (Total Graphics Power), versus Nvidia’s previous gen RTX 30 and 20 series offering. In short, we’ve got more shader compute TFLOPs, a lot more ray tracing and tensor core (machine learning) TFLOPs at a lot less average gaming power and board power. However, beyond that, Nvidia’s new GeForce RTX 4060 Ti also offers all the latest features of the company’s Ada GPU architecture and software, including DLSS 3 with frame generation and AVI 1 hardware encode support for game streaming and content creation. From a pricing standpoint, Nvidia is launching its new RTx 4060 midrange offering at basically the same price points as its previous generation, with partner OEM cards priced accordingly, depending on feature set and differentiating board-level features for each OEM.
When it comes to Founder Edition cards, like the new GeForce RTX 4060 Ti 8GB FE that my colleague Marco Chiappetta tested over at HotHardware (copious benchmarks), Nvidia has its own branded offering with an MSRP set identical to its previous-gen RTX 3060 Ti.
With its GeForce RTX 40 series, Nvidia was squarely focused on advancing PC gaming experiences not just with additional performance gains, but delivering those gains via advanced gaming and rendering technologies like DLSS 3, the company’s Deep Learning Super Sampling upscaling technology that is now equipped with AI-rendered frame generation for a significant lift in performance at very similar image quality to native rendering. As you’ll note, in this modern DX12 game engine, the GeForce RTX 4060 Ti slots in just behind a GeForce RTX 4070 but significantly ahead of a GeForce RTX 3070 Ti previous generation upper-midrange card. However, switch on DLSS 3 with frame gen (next chart down) and the new GeForce RTX 4060 Ti is actually on par with even a GeForce RTX 4070. Beyond this strong performance, this also speaks to a bit of optimization that Nvidia probably could use with respect to DLSS 3 in Returnal, since the RTX 4070 series clearly should be faster.
Moving on to F1 2022 with DLSS 3 or FSR enabled and we see the RTX 4060 Ti return to a second place finish, behind its beefier, more expensive RTX 4070 sibling, while enabling frame generation only extends Nvidia’s competitive lead even further over AMD’s previous-gen Radeon RX 6700 XT.
At $399, Nvidia’s new GeForce RTx 4060 Ti delivers solid PC gaming performance with 10 - 20% raw gains over the company’s previous generation RTX 3060 Ti, and much more available performance over its previous-gen RTX 3070 series when its advanced gaming features like DLSS 3 with Frame Generation are enabled. The company also delivers the 8GB variant of the card at identical price points to its previous generation, but with significantly lower power consumption characteristics, due to Nvidia’s more efficient Ada GPU architecture.
Detractors of Nvidia’s RTX 4060 Ti 8GB offering might point to its 8GB of VRAM and narrower 128-bit memory interface, the latter of which would have resulted in even more memory bandwidth if it was the same as the previous generation’s 192-bit memory interface. That said, as the saying goes, “the numbers don’t lie.” At much lower power consumption and significantly better performance when DLSS and frame generation are available, dropping in at the same midrange price band of $399, Nvidia’s new GeForce RTX 4060 Ti is a market leader currently.  In addition, with a higher-end $499 16GB and lower-end RTX 4060 standard variant waiting in the wings for launch this Summer, the company will continue to flesh out its Ada Lovelace gaming GPU arsenal with a wider offering of product options as well.
We’ll have to watch how AMD (and Intel) react to Nvidia’s new mainstream offering, and how their competitive next gen products might match up. Regardless, unless you want to step up to a higher GeForce RTX 40 or Radeon RX 7900 series price bracket, which will bring more performance still, the GeForce RTX 4060 Ti should prove to be a compelling option for a midrange gaming experience.
