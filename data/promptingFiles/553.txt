Nvidia RTX 3060 Vs RTX 3060 Ti: Which Should You Buy?

Feb 25, 2021, 09:00am EST

Today I'm reviewing Nvidia's (Nasdaq:NVDA) (GeForce RTX 3060 - Nvidia's cheapest RTX 3000-series Ampere graphics card to date. Of course, with stock levels, crypto mining and general pandemic issues all causing chaos in terms of prices, the MSRP of $329 is perhaps optimistic seeing as every other card in Nvidia's Ampere range has been selling for vastly higher prices because of these issues, or hasn't been available at all.
But seeing as this is the case for everything out there right now, we can only make comparisons with what we've got and today that means seeing how the new card compares to the RTX 3060 Ti and RTX 2060, its predecessor, and a card that Nvidia is reintroducing in the wake of dire stock levels and pricing.
There is one glimmer of hope, though, which is that Nvidia has curtailed the mining performance of the RTX 3060 on purpose to make it less appealing to would-be miners. In fact, the company is implementing a tamper-proof lockdown of the driver, RTX 3060 core and BIOS to halve mining performance. For those that are interested, I performed a quick test and saw a return of around 27MH/s mining Ethereum, which less than half what miners have reported from the RTX 3060 Ti. This means that other cards such as the AMD Radeon RX 5500 XT will likely be just as much if not more profitable and cheaper. It's not going to stop people using it for mining, but it's certainly less attractive than it might have been, especially compared to its predecessors.
This is a significantly less powerful card than the RTX 3060 Ti, with far fewer CUDA cores, Tensor cores and RT cores as well as a narrower 192-bit memory interface, compared to 256-bit for the RTX 3060 Ti. At first glance, the inclusion of 12GB memory seems overzealous given the RTX 3060 Ti, RTX 3070 and RTX 3080 all have less. However, this is down to the 192-bit memory interface. Each 32-bit chunk is tied to either 1GB or 2GB of GDDR6 memory. With the RTX 3060 Ti, the logical thing to do was to have 8GB, with 32 dividing into its 256-bit bus eight times. With a 192-bit memory interface, Nvidia had two choices - go with 6GB or 12GB and with AMD reportedly readying lower-end Radeon 6000-series cards with sizeable amounts of RAM, a 6GB RTX 3060 would have looked a little lacking. That's my theory anyway.
Nvidia doesn't make a Founders Edition model of the RTX 3060 so I used a Zotac RTX 3060 Twin Edge graphics card.
I used Nvidia's 461.64 release driver for the RTX 3060 testing along with a fully up-to-date version of Windows 10. The benchmarks focus on 1080p and 1440p as these are the resolutions potential customers of the RTX 3060 will be focussing on. However, I have performed additional testing at 4K in specific titles for comparison when using DLSS. The test system used an MSI MEG Z490 Ace, Intel Core i9-10900K, 16GB 3466MHz Corsair memory and an Intel 760p SSD along with a custom watercooling system.
At 1080p the RTX 3060 sat evenly between the RTX 2060 and RTX 3060 Ti, with the latter offering significantly more performance - around a 23 percent boost, while the new card was 21 percent quicker on the minimum 99th percentile frame rate than the aging RTX 2060. Stepping up to 1440p and it was a similar story - the RTX 3060 Ti offered 32 percent more performance while the RTX 3060 was 27 percent quicker than the RTX 3060.
With ray tracing and DLSS disabled, the minimum 99th percentile frame rate was 25 percent higher than the RTX 2060 at 1080p, but the RTX 3060 was much closer to the RTX 3060 Ti here with a deficit of just 15 percent. Enabling DLSS and ray tracing saw the performance advantage over the older card reduce to nine percent, but it was a similar situation with the RTX 3060 Ti, which was just eight percent faster.
With RTX features enabled at 1440p, the gap leaped up to a 21 percent boost over the RTX 2060 on the minimum 99th percentile frame rate, but the RTX 3060 Ti offered 29 percent more grunt here. Switching RTX features off, saw a 27 percent higher minimum 99th percentile than the RTX 2060, with the RTX 3060 Ti offering a 17 percent lead.

At 1080p it was another RTX 3060 sandwich, placed evenly between the RTX 3060 Ti and RTX 2060. The new card is a compelling upgrade over the 2060, but the RTX 3060 Ti gives you a lot more grunt for your cash.  The same was true at 1440p, with the RTX 3060 being the card with the lowest original retail price to top 50fps (53fps) on the minimum 99th percentile, while then RTX 2060 could only manage 44fps.
With RTX features off, at 1080p it was yet another evenly placed position for the RTX 3060, sitting squarely between the RTX 3060 Ti and RTX 2060, albeit with the RTX 3060 Ti offering less of a lead on the average frame rate.  At 1440p the RTX 3060 was a huge amount faster than the RTX 2060 with over 50% more performance and the RTX 3060 coming close to matching the mighty RTX 2080 too.
The really impressive result, though, was enabling ray tracing and DLSS, where at 4K, the RTX 3060 managed a playable 99th percentile of 28fps and average of 37fps, completely out gunning the RTX 2060 and even matching the Radeon RX 6800.

With ray tracing enabled, the RTX 3060 offered a massive lead over the RTX 2060 at 1080p in Metro Exodus, with a 99th percentile minimum frame rate of 34fps versus 26fps - a difference of 31 percent, sitting evenly between it and the RTX 3060 Ti. At 1440p it was less impressive with less than 10 percent between the cards and both falling back from the RTX 3060 Ti by some margin, with a similar story playing out if we disable RTX features at 1440p too.
The RTX 3060 saw a 40W lower peak system power draw than the RTX 3060 Ti and drew barely 20W more than the RTX 2060 for a whole lot more gaming performance. It should be noted that the Zotax RTX 3060 Twin Edge card I was using was barely audible throughout the tests too.
It's tricky to draw any conclusions from a graphics card launch at the moment as prices and stock levels are all over the place. Cutting the mining performance for the RTX 3060 was a good move as it's potentially turned the RTX 3060 from being a mining king into something mediocre, but given crypto prices right now, it's anyone's guess as to whether this will be enough to deter miners completely.
Actual performance is roughly where I expected it to be, offering a compelling. bordering on excellent upgrade from an RTX 2060 in most tests, with one or two exceptions, but with the RTX 3060 Ti offering noticeably more grunt, especially for 1440p gaming. The RTX 3060's ability to offer playable frame rates at 4K with DLSS enabled in some titles is also a handy feather to have in its cap, although again, if you'll be dabbling with 4K/DLSS then the RTX 3060 Ti is probably worth paying extra for.
Normally at this time we'd be saying goodbye to previous generations of cards and ushering in the RTX 3060 as the new low-mid range king, at least until we've seen AMD's equivalent in early March, but with the reintroduction of the RTX 2060 to appease gamers, these are strange times. Ultimately, it could be a very good card indeed and a particularly good upgrade for GTX 1060 and RTX 2060 owners. We can only hope prices sit at near normal levels and that you can actually buy one. If you do decide to go for it, Zotac's RTX 3060 Twin Edge is fantastic. It's compact, cool and extremely quiet.
