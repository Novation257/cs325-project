NVIDIA Unleashes Disruptive Ampere GPU Architecture And DGX A100 Supercomputers To Power Next-Gen AI Data Centers

May 14, 2020, 09:00am EDT

NVIDIA Founder and CEO Jensen Huang just delivered his GTC 2020 opening keynote and revealed the company’s new Ampere GPU Architecture, along with a powerful machine learning server platform – the NVIDIA DGX A100 — designed to accelerate data analytics, AI Training and Inference workloads.
NVIDIA is calling the newly announced DGX A100 "the world's most advanced system for all AI workloads" and claiming a single rack of five DGX A100 systems can replace an entire AI training and inference-focused data center, at 1/10th the cost, while using 1/20th the power and 1/25th the space. These are bold claims to be sure, but the Ampere A100 appears to be a massive leap forward, with immense GPU resources relative to previous-gen offerings.
Meet The Ampere-Based NVIDIA A100
At the heart of the NVIDIA DGX A100 is the new Ampere-based A100 GPU. The NVIDIA A100 is the single, largest chip currently being produced on a cutting-edge 7nm semiconductor manufacturing process. A single A100 is comprised of roughly 54 billion transistors, and features 3rd Generation Tensor cores, and NVLink and NVSwitch high-speed interconnect technologies. Versus the company’s previous-generation Volta architecture, NVIDIA claims a 20-fold increase in FP32 (Floating Point) Training and Int8 (Integer) Inference performance, and 2.5X the FP64 HPC performance. The A100 is capable of 312 TFLOPS of FP32 performance, 1,248 TOPS of Int8, and 19.5 TFLOPS of FP64 HPC throughput. NVIDIA’s Ampere architecture is also optimized for sparse AI Tensor Ops, and offers 2X faster execution overall versus the company’s previous generation Volta GPU architecture for machine learning applications.
NVIDIA also says the A100 GPU is the company’s first elastic, multi-instance GPU. New technology in the A100 allows it to support seven instances per GPU, for up to 7x higher throughput depending on the workload. With up to eight A100 GPUs in the DGX A100, there can be as many as 56 instances per system, which can allow NVIDIA’s customers to optimize computing power and resources for a diverse array of workloads and use-cases.
The NVIDIA DGX A100 3rd Generation Integrated AI System
Along with the A100 GPU, Huang also unveiled NVIDIA’s new DGX A100 server platform. The foundation of the DGX A100, is the NVIDIA HDX A100 integrated baseboard, which enables the DGX’s multi-GPU configurations. The NVIDIA DGX A100 is packed with eight, fully-connected NVIDIA A100 Tensor Core GPUs, with a total of 320GB of high-speed HBM2 memory. There are 12 NVIDIA NVLinks per GPU, which offer up to 600GB/s of inter-GPU bandwidth. And all of that GPU horsepower is accessible through six NVIDIA NVSwitches, which offer a massive 4.8TB/s of bidirectional bandwidth.
There is a ton of Mellanox silicon in the servers as well. On board are eight single-port Mellanox Connect-X6 cards for clustering (with 200GB/s of peak throughput), a dual-port ConnectX-6 for data/storage networking, and a Mellanox ConnectX-6 VPI HDR InfiniBand/200 GigE HCA. With multiple ultra-high speed, massively parallel processors like Ampere on board, you need dense, ultra-fast interconnects to feed the beast; this is where NVIDIA’s acquisition of Mellanox will pay dividends.
“NVIDIA DGX A100 is the ultimate instrument for advancing AI,” said Jensen Huang, founder and CEO of NVIDIA. “NVIDIA DGX is the first AI system built for the end-to-end machine learning workflow – from data analytics to training to inference. And with the giant performance leap of the new DGX, machine learning engineers can stay ahead of the exponentially growing size of AI models and data.”
An NVIDIA DGX A100 offers up to 10 PetaOPS of Int8 performance, 5 PFLOPS of FP16, 2.5 PFLOPS of TF32, and 156 TLOPS of FP64 compute performance. To put this in perspective, the previous-gen Volta-powered DGX-2 with 16 GPUs offers “only” 2 PetaFLOPS of mixed-precision performance.
Enter The NVIDIA DGX SuperPOD – Highly Connected Racks Of DGX A100
By combining 140 DGX A100 systems with NVIDIA Mellanox HDR 200Gbps InfiniBand interconnects, NVIDIA built its own DGX SuperPOD AI supercomputer, to handle advanced AI research workloads in areas like conversational AI, Genomics and Autonomous Driving.
Much in the same way multiple A100 GPUs can be paired together in a DGX A100 system, multiple DGX A100 systems can be linked to create a DGX SuperPOD. To aid customers that may want to assemble a similar A100-powered data center of their own, NVIDIA has also released a DGX SuperPOD reference architecture, which essentially gives customers a blueprint that follows the same design principles and best practices NVIDIA used to build its AI supercomputing DGX A100 cluster.
How To Score An NVIDIA A100
NVIDIA’s A100 GPU and DX100 Integrated AI System are already in production according to Mr. Huang, and are immediately available. DGX A100 systems have actually begun shipping already, with the first multi-system order going to the U.S. Department of Energy’s (DOE) Argonne National Laboratory. The DOE will be leveraging the new DGX A100 cluster to better understand and fight COVID-19, among other research efforts powered by high performance computing.
NVIDIA DGX A100 systems start at just $199,000.00 and are shipping now through NVIDIA Partner Network resellers. Due to their exponentially higher compute throughput per square millimeter, in the words of NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang, “the more you buy, the more you save.” However, what will be interesting to watch in the months ahead are how many NVIDIA DGX A100 systems enter the ranks of some of the fastest machine learning supercomputers in the world. With the performance and scalability of Ampere A100 and the DGX A100 platform, hyper-connected via Mellanox technologies, the results could be truly ground-breaking.
